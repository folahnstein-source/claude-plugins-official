# Vorsprung Teaser Structure — House Style Reference

Derived from Project Forest, Project Waterlock, Project Alphabet, and the EN_Teaser_Template.
All teasers follow a strict 2-page / 2-slide layout. Do not add sections, do not drop sections.

---

## PAGE 1 — Overview

### HEADER (top of page)
```
PROJECT [CODENAME]
[Tagline — one sentence investment thesis, e.g. "Investment opportunity in one of Germany's leading
manufacturers of water & hydraulic steelworks, supporting mission-critical infrastructure"]

                                                        [VORSPRUNG LOGO]

STRICTLY CONFIDENTIAL AND PROPRIETARY | Not an offer, no investment advice
```

### SUMMARY KPI BOX (top-left, compact table)

Present 5–7 key metrics in large bold type. Use this format:

```
EUR XX.Xm        Revenues FY[YY]A
XX%              Revenue CAGR (FY[YY]A–[YY]A)
XX%              Average EBITDA Margin
c. X.Xx          Acquisition Multiple
> EUR XXm        Current Order Intake   [if applicable]
```

Rules:
- Always include Revenue and EBITDA Margin
- Include CAGR only if ≥ 3 years of data available
- Include order backlog/intake if the business is project-based
- Use "c." for approximations
- State fiscal year reference for every figure

---

### COMPANY OVERVIEW (top-right, 5–7 bullet points)

Purpose: Orient the investor in 90 seconds — not just what the company does, but why it is defensible, what makes its revenue resilient, and who it serves.

Content (cover all that apply; every bullet must be grounded in a CIM fact):
- **Market position**: What the company is and its stated rank or scale within its niche (e.g., "one of the top 3 in DACH") — quote or paraphrase directly from the CIM
- **Business model**: Core segments or service lines (up to 3 sub-bullets); how the company earns revenue (project-based, recurring service, product sales, or mixed)
- **Revenue quality**: Share of recurring, contracted, or framework-agreement revenue; typical contract duration or renewal pattern if stated
- **Customer base**: Describe the customer mix by type (public/private sector, industry, geography) and note diversification or concentration where relevant — never name customers
- **Competitive moat**: The specific technical, regulatory, or reputational barrier that protects the company's position (certifications, reference requirements, specialist equipment, switching costs)
- **Operating footprint**: Generalised geography, number of sites, headcount (rounded); export share if meaningful
- **Succession and management**: Brief note on ownership/transition context and management commitment post-deal (anonymised)

Ordering rule: Lead with the market position claim. Follow with business model. End with moat and management.

Example (from Waterlock):
```
• Waterlock is one of the largest manufacturers of large-scale water and hydraulic steelworks
  as well as large component metalworking solutions in the DACH region.
• The company operates across two segments: hydraulic steelwork construction (~70% of revenue)
  and large-component metalworking (~30%), with a high share of publicly funded framework contracts
  providing multi-year revenue visibility.
• Customer base is predominantly public-sector authorities and federal infrastructure agencies,
  with no single customer exceeding ~25% of revenue — providing strong diversification.
• The company has completed various pioneering lighthouse projects, including some of Germany's
  technically most demanding waterlock construction projects, creating a reference library that
  new entrants cannot replicate.
• Very high barriers to entry: projects require specialist heavy-lifting equipment, decades of
  engineering references, and sector-specific approvals — limiting the qualified supplier pool
  to a handful of DACH incumbents.
• c. 250 FTEs across two production sites in Northern Germany; family-owned for over 50 years
  with senior management committed to a multi-year post-closing transition.
```

Prohibited: Management names, exact addresses, legal entity name, customer names.

### MARKET OVERVIEW (middle section, 5–6 bullets + 1 data visual)

Purpose: Establish the structural investment case. Cover market size, demand drivers, competitive structure, barriers, and the company's position within it — this section must answer "why now, why this market, why this company wins."

Content (cover all that apply; cite CIM page references for every quantified figure):
- **Market size and growth**: TAM with EUR value and CAGR; cite source explicitly (`[Source: CIM, p.X]` or name the research firm if quoted in CIM)
- **Structural demand drivers**: 2–3 specific, quantified tailwinds (regulatory mandates, infrastructure backlog, ESG requirements, demographic shifts) — avoid vague statements like "growing demand"; anchor every driver to a fact
- **Policy and funding backdrop**: Named programmes, government budgets, or mandated spending schedules that make demand visible and durable — include amounts and timelines where stated
- **Market structure and competitive landscape**: Approximate number of credible competitors; whether the market is fragmented (favours consolidation) or oligopolistic (favours incumbents); company's position within that structure (do not name competitors)
- **Barriers to entry**: Explain specifically why a new entrant cannot easily replicate the company's position — certifications, reference requirements, specialist equipment, long qualification cycles, regulatory approvals
- **Buy-and-build angle** (include only if CIM supports it): Estimated number of sub-scale targets in the market; typical revenue size; why consolidation creates margin or pricing uplift

Ordering rule: Open with market size. Follow with demand drivers and policy tailwinds. Close with competitive structure and barriers — this is where the defensibility argument lands.

Data visual: Include a table, timeline, or quantified breakdown illustrating one of the above (infrastructure backlog by year, funding budget by programme, or market share split). Label the source.

Example (from Waterlock):
```
• The hydraulic steelworks and lock-maintenance market in Germany represents a EUR 4.8bn+
  construction and maintenance opportunity through 2030, driven by a multi-decade backlog of
  ageing infrastructure [Source: CIM, p.14, citing BMVI data].
• 85% of the more than 600 locks and weirs in Germany are in inadequate condition; c. 15% are
  in acute need of renewal — federal authorities are legally mandated to restore navigable
  waterways, creating non-discretionary, publicly funded demand.
• The Federal Waterways and Shipping Administration (WSA) has approved EUR 3.5bn in maintenance
  budgets through 2030, with a further EUR 1.3bn earmarked for new construction — providing
  exceptional multi-year revenue visibility for qualified incumbents.
• Inland water navigation is growing as an ESG-compliant transport mode, emitting 33 gCO2/tkm
  versus 137 for road freight — increasing political and corporate demand for functional waterways.
• The market is highly oligopolistic: fewer than five DACH firms hold the engineering references,
  certifications, and heavy-lift equipment required to bid on major lock projects — new entrants
  face a 10–15 year qualification cycle before winning public-sector mandates.
• The fragmented maintenance and component sub-segment offers a buy-and-build opportunity:
  c. 20–30 regional metalwork fabricators with EUR 5–30m revenue serve adjacent infrastructure
  markets and could be consolidated under [Codename]'s platform [Source: CIM, p.22].
```

### REFERENCE PROJECTS (bottom of page 1, image grid)

Purpose: Demonstrate capability and scale without naming clients.

Format: 4–6 image placeholders, each with a 1-line anonymous caption.
Caption format: "[Project type], [Region]" — never "for [Client Name]".

Example:
```
Storm Surge Barrier, North-eastern Germany
Sea Lock, Northern Germany
Lock, Northern Germany
Floating Ferry, Northern Germany
```

If images are unavailable: `[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER: [project type] — to be provided by deal team]`

## PAGE 2 — Financials & Highlights

### FINANCIALS (top section, chart + 2–3 narrative bullets)

**Chart specification:**
- Dual-axis bar + line chart
- Left axis: Revenue (EUR m) and EBITDA (EUR m) as grouped or stacked bars
- Right axis: EBITDA Margin (%) as a line overlay
- X-axis: fiscal years, labelled FY[YY]A (actual) or FY[YY]FC / FY[YY]P (forecast)
- Historical years: darker fill. Forecast years: lighter fill or hatched
- Legend: Revenue | EBITDA | EBITDA Margin

In Markdown output, replace chart with:
```
[FINANCIAL CHART PLACEHOLDER]

| Year    | Revenue (EUR m) | EBITDA (EUR m) | EBITDA Margin (%) |
|---------|----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| FY20A   | X.X            | X.X            | X%                |
| FY21A   | X.X            | X.X            | X%                |
| FY22A   | X.X            | X.X            | X%                |
| FY23FC  | X.X            | X.X            | X%                |
| FY24P   | X.X            | X.X            | X%                |
```

**Narrative bullets (2–3, below chart):**
- Lead with the strongest financial fact (growth rate, margin level, or backlog)
- Second: quality of revenue (recurring, contracted, framework agreements)
- Third: forward visibility (forecasts already partially secured, order backlog)

Note at bottom if applicable: `Note: [FY]A figures normalised to exclude non-recurring items.`

### ORDER BACKLOG BREAKDOWN (optional, only if project-based business)

If the CIM contains order backlog by type or sector, include:
- Simple pie or bar breakdown (4–6 categories)
- 2 bullets: total backlog value + % from public/private sector

Skip this section entirely for businesses without project-based order books.

### INVESTMENT HIGHLIGHTS (6 items, icon grid)

Purpose: The core investment thesis — 6 reasons to invest, each grounded in CIM facts.

Format: 2 columns × 3 rows. Each item:
1. [ICON PLACEHOLDER] — use a category label in brackets: [ESG], [MARKET], [CUSTOMERS], [FINANCIALS], [BARRIERS], [TEAM], [GROWTH]
2. **Bold 3–6 word opening phrase** (the headline)
3. One to two sentences of supporting detail with at least one quantified fact

Rules:
- Exactly 6 highlights — not 5, not 7
- Bold opening phrase must stand alone as a scannable headline
- Each item ~25–40 words
- Cover diverse themes: don't put 3 financial items in a row

Example highlights (from Waterlock):
```
[ESG]      One-stop-shop with future-oriented product and service portfolio benefitting from two
           mega-trends **environment and infrastructure.**

[MARKET]   Operates in two **growth markets: hydraulic steelworks & flood protection.**

[BARRIERS] Very high barriers to entry through the necessity for references, reputation and
           relevant equipment.

[CUSTOMERS] Diverse long-standing and loyal base of **public and private customers.**

[GROWTH]   Steady growth over the past five years and high visibility on turnover through
           **record order backlog.**

[BRAND]    Highest reputation among hydraulic steelwork customers and track record of mastering
           technically **demanding lock constructions.**
```
